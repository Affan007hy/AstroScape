Solar System Overview

Our solar system, situated in the Orion Arm or Orion Spur of the Milky Way galaxy, is a complex celestial arrangement comprising the Sun, eight planets, five dwarf planets, over 290 moons, more than 1.3 million asteroids, and approximately 3,900 comets.

It navigates through the galaxy at an astounding speed of about 515,000 mph (828,000 kph), completing a full orbit around the galactic center approximately every 230 million years.



Named in honor of its central star, the Sun, the solar system is home to Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune as major planets, alongside dwarf planets such as Pluto, Ceres, Makemake, Haumea, and Eris.

Additionally, it hosts a multitude of moons and an extensive population of asteroids, comets, and meteoroids, making it a rich and dynamic cosmic environment.



Within the vast expanse of the Milky Way galaxy, the Sun, accompanied by its retinue of planets and dwarf planets, finds its place in the Local Interstellar Cloud within the Orion–Cygnus Arm.

Notable stellar neighbors include Proxima Centauri and Alpha Centauri, situated at distances of approximately 4.2465 and 4.344 light-years, respectively.

Among the planets, Neptune holds the record for the farthest known orbit, with a semi-major axis extending 30.11 astronomical units (4.5 billion kilometers or 2.8 billion miles) from the Sun.

Our solar system gracefully orbits the Galactic Center, inclined at an angle of 60.19°, at a distance roughly 27,000 ± 1,000 light-years, and moves through space at a velocity of 220 kilometers per second (136 miles per second).

The Sun, classified as a G2V type star, delineates its gravitational influence with a frost line marking approximately 5 astronomical units and a heliopause approximately 120 astronomical units away.
